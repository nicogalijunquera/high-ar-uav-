Project Overview

Why Longer Wings Make Better Drones

The idea in plain terms

Think of an albatross versus a sparrow — the albatross has long, narrow wings and can glide for thousands of kilometres with barely a wingbeat. The sparrow's short, stubby wings work fine for quick bursts, but are inefficient for sustained flight. The same physics applies to drone wings. Aspect ratio (wingspan divided by wing width) is the key number: a higher aspect ratio means less energy wasted fighting air resistance, which translates directly to longer flight times, greater range, and more payload capacity.

The Brunel UAS Society's 2022 competition drone has an aspect ratio of 6.3 — a reasonable starting point, but conservative compared to what's aerodynamically achievable. The IMechE UAS Challenge 2026 rewards efficiency, endurance, and technical innovation. This project asks: how much better can we make it, and does the structure hold up?

Research objective: Quantify the aerodynamic benefit of increasing wing aspect ratio on a fixed-area wing, then confirm the wing can still withstand 3.5 times the force of gravity — the competition's structural safety requirement.
What stayed the same: Wing area was kept constant at ≈ 1.0 m² across all designs. This means comparisons are fair — any efficiency gain comes purely from the shape change, not from making a bigger wing.

Theoretical Background

The Physics Behind the Design

The core idea — no maths required

When a wing generates lift, it also creates a penalty: induced drag — a swirling, energy-wasting vortex shed from each wingtip. The longer the wing relative to its width (higher aspect ratio), the weaker these vortices are, and the less energy is wasted. This is why gliders have very long, narrow wings. There is, however, a structural cost: a longer wing bends more under load, like a longer ruler flexing when you press on its end. The design challenge is finding the aspect ratio that maximises aerodynamic gain without the structure becoming dangerously heavy or fragile.

The chart shows why AR = 10 was chosen. Below AR ≈ 8, each unit of aspect ratio brings large efficiency gains — well worth the structural cost. Above AR ≈ 12, the aerodynamic curve flattens out but structural loads keep growing. AR = 10 captures +19.99% L/D improvement while keeping the root bending moment increase to a manageable +26%.

Why not AR = 15 or 20? Diminishing returns. Going from AR 10 to AR 20 would add structural complexity for a relatively small additional aerodynamic gain — and transport becomes impractical for a competition drone.
Induced drag in one sentence: It scales with 1/AR — so doubling the aspect ratio halves the induced drag. Going from AR 6.3 to 10 cuts the induced drag term by approximately 37%.

Methodology

Virtual Wind Tunnel — How the Simulations Work

What is CFD?

CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) is a virtual wind tunnel. Instead of building a physical model and testing it, we divide the air around the wing into millions of tiny cells and solve the equations of fluid motion on each one — using a computer to predict exactly how air flows, where drag comes from, and how much lift is generated. This approach lets us test many design variations quickly and cheaply, before committing to a physical prototype.

All simulations were performed in ANSYS Fluent. Both 2D cross-section (aerofoil profile) and full 3D wing simulations were run. Results were validated against published aerofoil data and checked for mesh independence before being used to inform design decisions.

Why SST k-ω turbulence model? It blends two established turbulence models: k-ω in the near-wall region (accurate for boundary layer separation and adverse pressure gradients) and k-ε in the freestream. This combination is well-suited for a cambered aerofoil operating near stall. The y⁺ ≈ 1 criterion ensures the first mesh cell sits inside the viscous sublayer — providing fully resolved boundary layers without relying on empirical wall functions.

3 Mesh Independence — Verifying the Results Are Trustworthy Validation▾

A key question in any CFD study: do the results depend on how fine the mesh is, or have we reached a level of detail where adding more cells makes no meaningful difference? This is called mesh independence.

Multiple mesh densities were tested. The final meshes were selected where the lift and drag coefficients changed by less than 1% with further refinement — confirming the results are reliable and not artificially influenced by mesh resolution.

4 Solver Convergence — Confirming a Stable Solution Validation▾

Convergence means the simulation has reached a stable, self-consistent solution — the numbers are no longer changing between iterations. Convergence was assessed on aerodynamic force coefficients (lift and drag), not just solver residuals, as this is a more physically meaningful criterion.

Criterion: less than 1% variation in CL and CD over the final 200 iterations.

Aerofoil Optimisation

Reshaping the Wing Profile for Less Drag

The concept, simply

The aerofoil is the cross-sectional shape of the wing — like a slice through it. Even small changes to this shape can meaningfully reduce drag. Instead of manually guessing which shape is best, we used an automated mathematical method that analyses the sensitivity of drag to every point on the surface simultaneously, then adjusts the shape iteratively until drag is minimised. The starting profile was the standard NACA 4415, widely used on competition drones.

The final optimised profile looks very similar to the original NACA 4415 to the naked eye — the change is subtle. But the improvement is real: +2.38% L/D from this step alone, on top of the aspect ratio gain.

  • Starting profile: NACA 4415 (4% max camber, 15% thickness).
  • Objective: minimise aerodynamic drag while maintaining lift.
  • The largest shape change is near the lower trailing edge — a subtle camber adjustment that reduces the adverse pressure gradient, delaying flow separation.
  • Optimisation done in 2D; the resulting profile was directly applied to the 3D wing.
  • L/D contribution: +2.38% (Model 2 → 4: L/D 20.39 → 20.79).
Limitation acknowledged: The optimisation was 2D only. A full 3D adjoint optimisation would capture spanwise effects and tip losses — estimated gain would be 2–3× higher. This is identified as the highest-priority future work.

2D Validation — Does the Simulation Match Reality?

Before trusting the optimisation results, the 2D simulation setup was validated against published experimental NACA 4415 data. The close agreement confirms the solver settings are correct.

A Technical Detail — Adjoint Method, Morphing Stages & Cp Analysis For engineers▾
Adjoint method (Jameson, 1988 [9]): A gradient-based optimisation technique that requires only two CFD solutions — a forward solve and an adjoint solve — to compute the sensitivity of the objective function (drag) to every surface point simultaneously, regardless of the number of design variables. This makes it computationally far more efficient than finite-difference sensitivity analysis for large numbers of design variables.

Winglet Design

Turning Wasted Vortex Energy into Efficiency

What do winglets actually do?

Look at the tips of any modern commercial aircraft and you will see the small upturned fins — winglets. At the end of each wing, pressure differences between the top and bottom surfaces cause air to spiral into a powerful vortex. This rotating column of air is a direct energy loss. Winglets disrupt the formation of these vortices, recovering some of that energy and making the wing behave aerodynamically as if it were physically longer. They were first studied by NASA's Richard Whitcomb in 1976 and are now standard on almost all commercial jets.

The winglet was designed as a modular plug-in — it slots into the wingtip rib and can be removed for transport without any structural modification to the main wing. This directly addresses the practicality requirements of the IMechE UAS Challenge 2026 competition.

  • L/D contribution from winglet: +4.05% (Model 4 → 5: 20.79 → 21.66).
  • Multiple cant angle and sweep configurations explored before finalising the design.
  • Modular interface: plug-in seats into the wingtip rib — removable without tools.
Two ways to measure the effective AR increase from a winglet:
Geometric formula (Whitcomb): AReff = 10.75
CFD equivalence (drag-matched): AReff = 10.34 ← used in this project
The CFD value is more conservative and physically meaningful — it accounts for real 3D interference and juncture drag that the geometric formula cannot capture.

Results

Performance Across All Five Wing Configurations

The headline result

Five wing designs were tested, each isolating one improvement. The baseline (BrUAS 2022) has a lift-to-drag ratio of 16.99 — meaning for every Newton of drag the wing produces, it generates 16.99 Newtons of lift. The final optimised wing achieves 21.66 — 27.51% more efficient. In practical terms, this translates directly to longer flight time, greater range, and more payload for the same motor and battery.

Structural Analysis

Does the Longer Wing Hold Together?

The structural challenge

A longer wing bends more under load — just like a longer ruler deflects more when you press on its end. Increasing the aspect ratio from 6.3 to 10 makes the wing 26% more stressed at the root (where it joins the fuselage). The question is: does the existing wing structure handle this increase, or do we need to redesign it? The answer turned out to be a targeted upgrade at just two critical positions, rather than a full structural redesign.

The wing must withstand 3.5 G competition loads — 3.5 times the force of gravity. To size the structure conservatively, the most demanding load case was assumed: an elliptic lift distribution, which is both the aerodynamically optimal spanwise load and the one that produces the highest root bending moment.

  • Root shear force V(0) = 67.5 N — the maximum shear at Rib 1.
  • Root bending moment: +26% increase over BrUAS 2022 due to AR increase (√AR scaling).
  • BrUAS 2022 structural failure moment ≈ 433 Nm — provides the design limit reference.
  • Only Ribs 1 and 13 needed upgrading to plywood. All 15 remaining ribs stay as balsa.

Why beam theory?

Analytical beam theory (Megson [8]) was used rather than finite element analysis (FEA). Beam theory is conservative, well-validated for slender wing structures, and appropriate for a preliminary design study. Full FEA is identified as a priority before physical manufacture — this is a known limitation of the current analysis.
Conclusions & Future Work

What Was Found, What Comes Next

Summary for everyone

This project proved that a longer wing makes a meaningfully better drone — specifically, 27.51% more aerodynamically efficient than the current Brunel competition wing. The improvement came from three additive design changes: making the wing longer (the biggest single gain), computationally optimising the wing cross-section profile, and adding modular winglets at the tips. The structural analysis confirmed the wing can handle competition loads with targeted reinforcement at just two rib positions — no major redesign required.

Key Findings

  • Increasing AR from 6.3 to 10 on a constant-area wing delivers +19.99% L/D — the dominant performance driver. New geometry: b = 3180 mm, c = 317 mm.
  • Adjoint-based aerofoil optimisation adds +2.38%, with the primary shape change at the lower trailing edge reducing the adverse pressure gradient.
  • A modular plug-in winglet contributes +4.05%. AReff = 10.34 (CFD equivalence) preferred over the geometric estimate of 10.75.
  • Combined total improvement: +27.51% — L/D rises from 16.99 to 21.66.
  • Structural viability confirmed: only Ribs 1 and 13 need plywood; all 15 remaining ribs stay as balsa. The +26% root bending moment increase is accommodated by targeted material changes only.
  • Modular winglet design satisfies competition transport and assembly constraints (IMechE UAS Challenge 2026 [5]).

Limitations & Future Work

  • 2D aerofoil optimisation only: Full 3D adjoint optimisation would capture spanwise effects — estimated gain 2–3× higher. This is the highest-priority future work.
  • No experimental validation: Wind tunnel testing is needed to confirm CFD predictions. Ma & Elham [7] validates the method, not the specific geometry.
  • Single operating condition: All CFD at α = 1°, V = 31.5 m/s. Off-design performance (range of angles, gusts, climb) not fully characterised.
  • Analytical structural analysis only: Full finite element analysis (FEA) is recommended before manufacture.
  • Physical prototype and flight test: Required to close the validation loop for IMechE competition entry.
  • Combined optimisation: Simultaneous 3D aerofoil + winglet optimisation may reveal synergistic gains not captured by the sequential approach.
